\begin{abstract}

  Aerial manipulations using aerial robots are gaining attention for their work in high places and hazardous locations. 
  While autonomous control of robots has advanced, teleoperation by human operators is still necessary for tasks in complex and unknown environments. 
  In such teleoperation, haptic feedback that allows the operator to perceive the forces exerted on the robot is important. 
  In many cases, underactuated multirotors are used for teleoperation, but the use of fully actuated multirotors is advancing for more complex tasks. 
  Conventional teleoperation methods cannot achieve both intuitive control corresponding to the increased degrees of freedom and feedback of all 6-dimensional force and torque. 
  Therefore, in this study, we propose an operating device capable of expressing 6-dimensional force and torque, and a teleoperation system that makes it easy to perform work remotely using a fully actuated multi-rotor. 
  The operating device has a floating base with a thrust and vectoring mechanism, enabling independent control of position and attitude and independent presentation of forces and torques. 
  The teleoperation system has Position Mapping Mode and Velocity Mapping Mode settings, enabling both rapid movement in a wide area and precise movement for work. 
  We verified the effectiveness of the proposed method for aerial telemanipulations through experiments cleaning tilted walls. 
  Our new framework contributes to the realization of more complex aerial tasks using aerial robots.
  
\end{abstract}

\section{Teleoperation Device}\label{sec:teleop_device}

\subsection{Design of the Remote Manipulation Device}\label{sec:device_hardware}
This section describes the design methodology of the proposed remote manipulation device, which achieves both independent presentation of a six-degree-of-freedom (6-DoF) wrench and compatibility with an ungrounded configuration. % as discussed in \secref{sec:related_works_device}%

\subsubsection{Required Elements for Remote Manipulation Devices}

The remote manipulation device proposed in this study enables an operator to perform contact tasks through a robot located at a distance. 
Many contact tasks executed by humans involve grasping and manipulating tools. 
The operator holds the handle of a tool, manipulates it, and receives reaction forces from the target object through the handle. 
In such tasks, the human and the work object interact via the handle.

To maximize intuitiveness in robotic teleoperation during contact tasks, both motion input and force feedback equivalent to directly grasping a tool are required. 
Therefore, the teleoperation device should replicate the state of a tool handle. 
A tool handle is characterized by (i) the presence of a longitudinal axis suitable for grasping and (ii) the absence of fixation to any object other than the human hand. 
% The latter characteristic corresponds to the ungrounded configuration discussed in \secref{sec:related_works_device}. 
Accordingly, the proposed device must satisfy these two characteristics.

To realize this concept, the device must generate a wrench without being fixed to the external environment. 
Methods for wrench generation in an ungrounded manner include inertial forces, electromagnetic forces, and thrust. 
Devices utilizing inertial forces include the use of flywheel counter-torque generated by rotation \cite{itorqu} and the use of mass transfer mechanisms \cite{shifty}. 
However, these approaches are limited to presenting forces or torques in only one or two dimensions. 
It is unclear whether they can independently present all six components of a wrench. 

Electromagnetic force is governed by the inverse square law with respect to distance, which makes it difficult to realize a fully ungrounded configuration. 
In contrast, thrust can generate force in an ungrounded manner using relatively simple mechanisms such as high-speed gas jets or rotating propellers. 
Furthermore, in the field of aerial robotics, methods for independently generating six-dimensional wrenches using thrust have been well established. 
Therefore, the use of thrust is considered appropriate for achieving the objective of this study.

In typical human contact tasks, reaction forces applied to the fingertips range from several newtons to more than ten newtons. 
Rotating propellers are commonly used to generate forces of this magnitude at the scale of handheld tools (approximately 1\,m). 
When installing propellers on a handheld device, preventing contact between the operator’s body and the propellers is critically important for safety. 
Therefore, the propellers must be enclosed.

This study aims to independently generate all six components of a wrench. 
To achieve independent 6-DoF wrench generation using thrust, at least six control inputs are required. 
Two general thrust configurations are commonly adopted: (i) arranging six or more tilted rotors, or (ii) deflecting the thrust direction using additional actuators. 
The former has a simpler structure but is inefficient in generating thrust in the gravitational direction to compensate for the device’s weight. 
The latter requires a more complex mechanism but allows optimal thrust direction, resulting in higher thrust efficiency. 
Since contact tasks often require sustained operation, extending operational duration is essential. 
Improving thrust efficiency is therefore critical, and the thrust deflection mechanism is adopted in this study.

\subsubsection{Airframe Design of the Remote Manipulation Device}\label{subsec:device_design}

An overview of the proposed device that satisfies the above requirements is shown in \ref{fig:device_design}.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.90\columnwidth]{figs/device/device_overview}
  \caption{Overview of the proposed device design}
  \label{fig:device_design}
\end{figure}

The main body of the device consists of an elongated rod that mimics a tool handle. 
A thrust deflection mechanism is installed at each end of the rod, as shown on the right side of \ref{fig:device_design}. 
This mechanism is a modification of the one proposed by Zhao et al.~\cite{DRAGON}. 

An electric ducted fan (EDF) is mounted at each end of the deflection mechanism, as shown at the bottom of \ref{fig:device_design}. 
The duct structure physically prevents contact between the operator’s body and the propeller. 
In addition, the duct provides a rectifying effect that improves thrust efficiency, thereby contributing to longer operational time.

Each deflection mechanism has two rotational degrees of freedom orthogonal to the longitudinal axis of the device. 
The EDFs at both ends can independently control their thrust magnitudes. 
Therefore, each thrust deflection module has four control degrees of freedom: two rotational and two thrust-related. 
Since two such modules are installed, the entire device has eight control degrees of freedom. 
Because this exceeds the required six degrees of freedom, the device can redundantly and independently present a six-dimensional wrench.

\subsection{Control of the Remote Manipulation Device}\label{sec:device_control}

This section describes the method for determining each control input to realize a desired wrench with the proposed device. The following variables are defined:

\begin{itemize}
  \item $\{W\}$ : reference coordinate system  
  \item $\{C\}$ : handle coordinate system  
  \item $^{\{W\}}R_{\{C\}}$ : rotation matrix from the reference coordinate system to the handle coordinate system  
  \item $^{\{C\}}\bm{f}$ : resultant force of the rotor thrusts expressed in the handle coordinate system  
  \item $^{\{C\}}\bm{\tau}$ : resultant torque of the rotor thrusts expressed in the handle coordinate system  
  \item $\lambda_i$ : thrust generated by the $i$-th rotor  
  \item $^{\{C\}}\bm{p}_i$ : position vector of the $i$-th rotor expressed in the handle coordinate system  
  \item $\theta_i$ : deflection angle of the $i$-th mechanism about the $x$-axis of the handle coordinate system  
  \item $\phi_i$ : deflection angle of the $i$-th mechanism about the $y$-axis of the handle coordinate system  
\end{itemize}

As described above, the proposed device has eight control degrees of freedom, as shown in the upper part of \ref{fig:control_line_graph}. 
A system including thrust deflection is inherently nonlinear. As proposed in \cite{nishio}, both thrust magnitude and deflection angles can be determined by solving a nonlinear optimization problem. 
However, solving such nonlinear optimization problems is computationally expensive and significantly increases the control cycle time when using compact onboard computers.

To address this issue, Kamel et al.~\cite{kamel} proposed a linearization method for thrust vectoring under the assumption that the deflection angle changes sufficiently rapidly and that the thrust effect during angle transitions can be neglected. 
This study adopts this linearization approach to determine both thrust magnitudes and deflection angles. 
In addition, the counter-torque generated by rotor rotation is sufficiently small compared with the torque generated by thrust and is therefore neglected.

\subsubsection{Determination of Thrust Angles Using Virtual Thrust Allocation}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.90\columnwidth]{figs/device/control_line_graph_v3}
  \caption{Top: Control degrees of freedom of the proposed device. The black lines indicate the handle coordinate system fixed to the device. Bottom: Virtual thrust model used for control.}
  \label{fig:control_line_graph}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.90\columnwidth]{figs/device/thrust_image}
  \caption{Left: Thrust vector represented by deflection angles. Right: Decomposition into three-dimensional virtual translational thrust components.}
  \label{fig:thrust_image}
\end{figure}

Each thrust deflection module has four control inputs (two thrust magnitudes and two rotational angles), and the entire device has eight control degrees of freedom including the rotations shown in the upper part of \ref{fig:control_line_graph}. 

Assuming equal thrust on the left and right sides of each module ($\lambda_1 = \lambda_2$, $\lambda_3 = \lambda_4$), the reachable space of the resultant thrust vector forms a sphere centered at the module’s rotation center, as shown on the left side of \ref{fig:thrust_image}. 
This polar-coordinate representation introduces nonlinearity and increases computational cost. 
Therefore, as shown on the right side of \ref{fig:thrust_image}, the reachable thrust space is decomposed into orthogonal components of a three-dimensional virtual translational thrust. 
With this treatment, the system including the deflection angles can be handled as a linear system.

By introducing the virtual thrust $\bm{\lambda}_{\mathrm{v}} = \{\bm{\lambda}_{\mathrm{v}1}, \bm{\lambda}_{\mathrm{v}2}\}$, the control inputs are reduced from eight dimensions to six translational degrees of freedom, as shown in the lower part of \ref{fig:control_line_graph}. 
The reduction of two degrees of freedom results from the constraint of equal left–right thrust within each module. 
Under this assumption, torque about the $x$-axis of the handle coordinate system $\{C\}$ cannot be generated. 
The treatment of this constraint is discussed in the next subsection.

The resultant force and torque generated by the virtual thrust in the handle coordinate system are expressed as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:v_force_total}
  ^{\{C\}}\bm{f} = \sum_{i=1}^{2} \bm{\lambda}_{\mathrm{v}i},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:v_torque_total}
  ^{\{C\}}\bm{\tau} = \sum_{i=1}^{2} \bm{\lambda}_{\mathrm{v}i} \times ^{\{C\}}\bm{p}_{\mathrm{v}i}.
\end{equation}

Combining these equations,
\begin{equation}
  ^{\{C\}}\bm{w}
  =
  \begin{pmatrix}
  ^{\{C\}}\bm{f} \\
  ^{\{C\}}\bm{\tau}
  \end{pmatrix}
  =
  Q_1 \bm{\lambda}_{\mathrm{v}},
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
  Q_1 =
  \begin{pmatrix}
  E_3 & E_3 \\
  \mathrm{skew}(\bm{p}_{\mathrm{v}1}) & \mathrm{skew}(\bm{p}_{\mathrm{v}2})
  \end{pmatrix}
  \in \mathbb{R}^{6 \times 6}.
\end{equation}
Here, $E_3 \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3}$ is the identity matrix, and $\mathrm{skew}(\bm{p})$ denotes the skew-symmetric matrix of $\bm{p}$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{skew}(\bm{p}) =
  \begin{pmatrix}
  0 & -p_z & p_y \\
  p_z & 0 & -p_x \\
  -p_y & p_x & 0
  \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
In this virtual thrust configuration, torque about the $x$-axis of $\{C\}$ cannot be generated, and the fourth row of $Q_1$ consists of zeros.

Given the desired wrench $\bm{w}_{\mathrm{des}}$ in the reference coordinate system, the virtual thrust is determined as
\begin{equation}
  \bm{\lambda}_{\mathrm{v}}
  =
  Q_1^{\#}
  \, ^{\{W\}}R_{\{C\}}
  \left(
  \bm{w}_{\mathrm{des}} + \bm{g}
  \right),
\end{equation}
where $\bm{g} = (0,\,0,\,Mg,\,0,\,0,\,0)^\top$ is the gravitational wrench due to the device’s weight, and $Q_1^{\#}$ denotes the pseudoinverse of $Q_1$.

The deflection angles are then determined as
\begin{equation}
  \theta_i = \tan^{-1}\!\left( -\frac{\lambda_{\mathrm{v}iy}}{\lambda_{\mathrm{v}iz}} \right),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \phi_i = \tan^{-1}\!\left(
  \frac{\lambda_{\mathrm{v}ix}}
  {-\lambda_{\mathrm{v}iy}\sin\theta_i + \lambda_{\mathrm{v}iz}\cos\theta_i}
  \right).
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Determination of Thrust Magnitudes}

The relationship between the virtual thrust magnitudes and the actual rotor thrusts $\bm{\lambda} = \{\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3, \lambda_4\}$ is given by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:thrust_sum_v1}
  \lvert \bm{\lambda}_{\mathrm{v}1} \rvert = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:thrust_sum_v2}
  \lvert \bm{\lambda}_{\mathrm{v}2} \rvert = \lambda_3 + \lambda_4.
\end{equation}

Although torque about the $x$-axis cannot be generated in the virtual thrust model, it can be generated in the actual thrust configuration through the thrust difference between the left and right rotors. 
The torque about the $x$-axis is expressed as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:x_torque}
  \tau_x
  =
  \sum_{k=1}^{2}
  \left(
  d_{2k-1}\lambda_{2k-1}
  -
  d_{2k}\lambda_{2k}
  \right)
  \cos\phi_k,
\end{equation}
where $d_i$ denotes the perpendicular distance of each rotor from the $x$-axis.

Combining \eqref{eq:thrust_sum_v1}–\eqref{eq:x_torque},
\begin{equation}
  \begin{pmatrix}
  \lvert \bm{\lambda}_{\mathrm{v}1} \rvert \\
  \lvert \bm{\lambda}_{\mathrm{v}2} \rvert \\
  \tau_x
  \end{pmatrix}
  =
  Q_2 \bm{\lambda},
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
  Q_2 =
  \begin{pmatrix}
  1 & 0 & 1 & 0 \\
  0 & 1 & 0 & 1 \\
  d_1\cos\phi_1 & -d_2\cos\phi_1 & d_3\cos\phi_2 & -d_4\cos\phi_2
  \end{pmatrix}
  \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 4}
\end{equation}
is the thrust allocation matrix.

The target torque about the $x$-axis is defined as
\begin{equation}
  \tau_x = \tau_{\mathrm{des}x} + \tau_{\mathrm{counter}},
\end{equation}
where $\tau_{\mathrm{counter}}$ is the counter-torque generated by actuation of the deflection mechanism about the $x$-axis. 
Using the moment of inertia $I_{\mathrm{module}}$ of the thrust deflection module about the $x$-axis,
\begin{equation}
  \tau_{\mathrm{counter}} = \sum_{i=1}^{2} I_{\mathrm{module}} \ddot{\theta}_i.
\end{equation}

Finally, the thrust magnitudes of each rotor are determined as
\begin{equation}
  \bm{\lambda}
  =
  Q_2^{\#}
  \begin{pmatrix}
  \lvert \bm{\lambda}_{\mathrm{v}1} \rvert \\
  \lvert \bm{\lambda}_{\mathrm{v}2} \rvert \\
  \tau_{\mathrm{des}x} + \tau_{\mathrm{counter}}
  \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
where $Q_2^{\#}$ denotes the pseudoinverse of $Q_2$.

\subsection{Generation of Operating Commands for the Robot}\label{sec:operating_command_generator}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=1.0\columnwidth]{figs/architecture/command_overview}
  \caption{Overview of operating command generation.}
  \label{fig:command_overview}
\end{figure}

This section describes the method for controlling the position and orientation of the robot end-effector, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:command_overview}.  

Robots to be controlled can be broadly classified into two types: those with a fixed base and a finite end-effector workspace, and those with a mobile base and an effectively infinite end-effector workspace.  
Although the operator may be mobile, in practical remote operation scenarios the operator’s motion is constrained, resulting in a finite control space.  

Therefore, operating command generation requires:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Mapping from a finite control space to a finite end-effector workspace  
  \item Mapping from a finite control space to an infinite end-effector workspace  
\end{itemize}

By appropriately combining these mappings, various remote end-effector tasks using different types of robots can be realized.

\subsubsection{Mapping from a Finite Control Space to a Finite End-Effector Workspace}\label{subsec:posmode}

When both the control space and the end-effector workspace are finite, a mapping of equal dimensionality can be defined from the device position and orientation to the robot end-effector position and orientation.

The target position $\bm{p}_{\text{target}}$ and target orientation $\bm{\theta}_{\text{target}}$ of the robot end-effector are determined from the device position $\bm{p}_{\text{device}}$ and orientation $\bm{\theta}_{\text{device}}$ as follows:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta \bm{p}_{\text{device}} = \bm{p}_{\text{device}}(t) - \bm{p}_{\text{device}}(t_0)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \Delta \bm{\theta}_{\text{device}} = \bm{\theta}_{\text{device}}(t) - \bm{\theta}_{\text{device}}(t_0)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \bm{p}_{\text{target}} = \bm{p}_{\text{robot}}(t_0) + \bm{k}_1 \odot \Delta \bm{p}_{\text{device}}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \bm{\theta}_{\text{target}} = \bm{\theta}_{\text{device}}(t)
\end{equation}
where $t_0$ denotes the start time of operation.  
The operator $\odot$ represents the Hadamard product, i.e., element-wise multiplication of vectors with identical dimensions.

During manual task execution, the operator plans the trajectory of hand motion.  
Accordingly, for position mapping, the displacement vector from the device initial position is mapped to the displacement vector from the robot initial position, enabling intuitive operation.  
By appropriately setting the scaling factor $\bm{k}_1$, the method can accommodate differences in workspace size among robots.

For orientation mapping, however, no scaling is applied.  
This is because rotational quantities cannot generally be treated as linear variables in the same manner as translational positions, and simple scaling may significantly degrade operational intuitiveness.

\subsubsection{Mapping from a Finite Control Space to an Infinite End-Effector Workspace}\label{subsec:velmode}

When the end-effector workspace is effectively infinite, direct position mapping as described above is not suitable.  
Therefore, this study proposes a method that maps the device position and orientation to the target linear velocity and angular velocity of the robot end-effector.

The target linear velocity $\dot{\bm{p}}_{\text{target}}$ and target angular velocity $\dot{\bm{\theta}}_{\text{target}}$ are defined as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:velmapping_pos}
  \dot{\bm{p}}_{\text{target}} = \bm{k}_2 \odot \Delta \bm{p}_{\text{device}}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:velmapping_ang}
  \dot{\bm{\theta}}_{\text{target}} = \bm{k}_3 \odot \Delta \bm{\theta}_{\text{device}}
\end{equation}
where $\bm{k}_2$ and $\bm{k}_3$ are scaling factors.

Because the operator’s angular input variations are confined to a small range, Euler angles can be regarded as locally linear, allowing scaling to be applied.  
For sufficiently small angular displacements, the differential of Euler angles can be approximated as angular velocity in a first-order sense.

By mapping device displacement to robot velocity as in Eqs.~(\ref{eq:velmapping_pos}) and (\ref{eq:velmapping_ang}), the controllable space is extended from finite to effectively infinite.  
This enables full utilization of the infinite workspace of base-mobile robots.  
This approach is analogous to velocity-based control using conventional joystick-type devices.

\subsection{Feedback Wrench Generation}\label{sec:feedback_wrench_generator}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=1.0\columnwidth]{figs/architecture/feedback_overview}
  \caption{Overview of feedback wrench generation.}
  \label{fig:feedback_overview}
\end{figure}

This section describes the method for generating candidate feedback wrenches $\bm{w}_{\text{cand}}$ applied to the device, as illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:feedback_overview}.

The target feedback wrench is composed of the following three components:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Feedback $\bm{w}_{\text{ref}}$ for recognition of the device origin during target velocity command generation  
  \item Feedback $\bm{w}_{\text{obs}}$ for obstacle avoidance  
  \item Feedback $\bm{w}_{\text{env}}$ for recognition of the wrench acting on the robot end-effector  
\end{itemize}

Each feedback component is described in detail below.  
The operator selects which feedback components to activate depending on the operational scenario and required functionality.  
When multiple feedback components are used simultaneously, their sum is output:
\begin{equation}
  \bm{w}_{\text{cand}} = \bm{w}_{\text{ref}} + \bm{w}_{\text{obs}} + \bm{w}_{\text{env}}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Feedback for Origin Recognition During Target Velocity Command Generation}\label{subsec:feedback_in_velmode}

In the velocity mapping described in Sec.~\ref{subsec:velmode}, the robot target velocity is determined based on the displacement from the device position at the start of operation (hereafter referred to as the device origin).  
If the operator cannot accurately perceive the device origin, motion in the intended direction becomes difficult.

In conventional joystick-type devices, a spring attached to the base generates a restoring force toward the origin, which is transmitted to the operator’s fingers.

The proposed device has a six-degree-of-freedom wrench presentation capability using thrust.  
This capability is utilized to convey information about the device origin to the operator.  
Since the control command varies according to displacement from the device origin, it is appropriate that the feedback wrench also varies with this displacement.

However, if a purely proportional relationship is used, the presented wrench becomes small near the origin, making it difficult for the operator to recognize the device position at which the robot remains stationary.  
To address this issue, a logarithmic transformation is applied to each element of the wrench so that the presented wrench increases near the origin.

\begin{equation}
  \begin{cases}
    \bm{f}_{\text{ref}} = - \bm{k}_4 \odot \log (\Delta \bm{p}_{\text{device}} + \bm{b}) & (\Delta \bm{p}_{\text{device}} \geq 0) \\
    \bm{f}_{\text{ref}} = \bm{k}_4 \odot \log ((-\Delta \bm{p}_{\text{device}}) + \bm{b}) & (\Delta \bm{p}_{\text{device}} < 0)
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \begin{cases}
    \bm{\tau}_{\text{ref}} = - \bm{k}_5 \odot \log (\Delta \bm{\theta}_{\text{device}} + \bm{b}) & (\Delta \bm{\theta}_{\text{device}} \geq 0) \\
    \bm{\tau}_{\text{ref}} = \bm{k}_5 \odot \log (-\Delta \bm{\theta}_{\text{device}} + \bm{b}) & (\Delta \bm{\theta}_{\text{device}} < 0)
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\bm{k}_4$ and $\bm{k}_5$ are scaling factors, and $\bm{b} = (1, 1, 1)^\top$.

\subsubsection{Feedback for Obstacle Avoidance}\label{subsec:feedback_from_obstacle}

During operation of a mobile robot, unintended contact with obstacles other than the intended work object must be avoided to prevent accidents.  
Although the operator can avoid obstacles based on visual feedback, obstacles located in blind spots may be overlooked.

To mitigate this risk, we propose a method that provides haptic feedback based on the distance between the robot and nearby obstacles, thereby assisting the operator in reliably avoiding contact.

Let $\bm{p}_{\text{wall}}$ denote the position of the nearest obstacle.  
The distance between the robot position $\bm{p}$ and the obstacle is given by:
\begin{equation}
  d = |\bm{p} - \bm{p}_{\text{wall}}|
\end{equation}

A potential function dependent on the distance $d$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  a = \frac{1}{d_0} \left( \frac{1}{\sqrt{\varepsilon}} - 1 \right)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  U(\bm{p}) = \frac{U_{\max}}{1 + a d}
\end{equation}
where $d_0$ is the distance at which the gradient magnitude becomes sufficiently small (equal to $\varepsilon$), and $U_{\max}$ determines the maximum value of the potential.

The gradient of this potential field at position $\bm{p}$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \nabla U(\bm{p}) = -\frac{a U_{\max}}{(1 + a d)^2} \frac{\bm{p} - \bm{p}_{\text{wall}}}{d}
\end{equation}

The feedback wrench is then generated as:
\begin{equation}
  \bm{w}_{\text{obs}} = \bm{k}_6 \odot \nabla U(\bm{p})
\end{equation}
where $\bm{k}_6$ is a scaling factor.

This feedback wrench increases as the robot approaches the obstacle, thereby guiding the operator to avoid contact.

\subsubsection{Feedback for Recognition of the Wrench Acting on the Robot End-Effector}\label{subsec:feedback_from_robot}

As described in Sec.~\ref{chap:introduction}, when performing contact tasks via a remotely operated robot, it is essential for the operator to perceive the wrench acting on the robot end-effector.

When the wrench $\bm{w}_{\text{measured}}$ at the robot end-effector is obtained through measurement or estimation, the feedback wrench is generated as:
\begin{equation}
  \bm{w}_{\text{env}} = \bm{k}_7 \odot \bm{w}_{\text{measured}}
\end{equation}
where $\bm{k}_7$ is a scaling factor determined according to the maximum wrench that the robot end-effector can exert and the minimum wrench magnitude that the operator can perceive.

\subsection{Physical Configuration of the Remote Control Device}

\subsubsection{Hardware Configuration}
The hardware configuration of the remote control device, constructed based on the method proposed in this research, is shown in \ref{fig:device_picture}.
Each module is summarized in \ref{tab:device_module}.
The hardware enclosure was primarily constructed using CFRP and PLA.
The Spinal flight controller, a custom-built control board, incorporates sensors such as an inertial measurement unit (IMU) and a processor unit (STM32 H7).

This section describes the verification experiment conducted to evaluate whether the constructed remote control device can independently generate a six-degree-of-freedom (6-DoF) wrench.
The experimental setup and results are presented and discussed below.

The experimental setup is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:device_experiment_overview}.  
A \textit{Leptorino PFS055YA501U6} six-axis force/torque sensor is mounted in the region outlined in blue in the figure.

Figure~\ref{fig:device_wrench_plot_all} shows the target wrench and the measured wrench.  
Because the raw sensor signals contain measurement noise, the plotted measured values correspond to signals processed using a low-pass filter.  
Table~\ref{tab:device_wrench_RMSE} summarizes the root mean square error (RMSE) between the target wrench and the measured wrench.

  \label{fig:device_wrench_plot_all}
\end{figure}

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{RMSE between the target wrench and the measured wrench}
  \begin{tabular}{cccccc} \hline
    $F_x[\si{N}]$ & $F_y[\si{N}]$ & $F_z[\si{N}]$ & $T_x[\si{Nm}]$ & $T_y[\si{Nm}]$ & $T_z[\si{Nm}]$ \\ \hline

\end{table}

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:device_wrench_plot_all}, the six-dimensional wrench is generally generated in a decoupled manner.  
However, for the translational forces in the $x$ and $y$ directions, a consistent offset is observed between the target and measured values.  
This offset is presumed to be caused by slack in the timing belt of the thrust deflection mechanism.  
Because the timing belt is made of a flexible material that can stretch, a discrepancy may arise between the commanded servo motor angle driving the deflection mechanism and the actual deflection angle.  
This effect is also considered to contribute to the fluctuations observed in the translational forces when torques are generated.

In addition, small spikes are observed in each wrench component at the moments when the target wrench command changes.  
These spikes are attributed to the difference in response time between (i) the thrust variation caused by changes in rotor speed and (ii) the angular motion of the deflection mechanism driven by the servo motor.  
The time required for the deflection mechanism to complete its angular displacement is longer than the time required for the rotor to reach the commanded speed.  
Consequently, when the target wrench changes, the thrust variation occurs first, followed by the rotation of the deflection mechanism.  
The transient wrench generated during this interval appears as spikes in the measured signals.

Although the six-degree-of-freedom wrench generation is not perfectly decoupled, the principal contribution of the proposed force feedback device—namely, the realization of six-dimensional wrench presentation using an angled ground system—has been successfully demonstrated.

  \begin{tabular}{c|cc} \hline

The ideal trajectory shown by the black dashed line in \ref{fig:beetle_obstacle_plot} represents the safest path that maximizes the distance from the obstacle.  
The coverage rate shown in \ref{tab:beetle_obstacle_trajectory} quantifies the proportion of the ideal trajectory achieved during operation and is defined as follows.

Let the ideal trajectory be represented by a continuous curve $\bm{P}(s)$ parameterized by arc length $s \in [0, L]$, and let the measured trajectory be $\{\bm{x}_k\}_{k=1}^{n}$.  
The closest point on the ideal trajectory to each measured point is defined as
\begin{equation}
  s_k^* = \arg \min_{s \in [0,L]} \left\| \bm{x}_k - \bm{P}(s) \right\|.
\end{equation}
The maximum arc length reached during operation is then
\begin{equation}
  s_{\text{max}} = \max_{k} s_k^*,
\end{equation}
and the coverage rate is calculated as
\begin{equation}
  C = \frac{s_{\text{max}}}{L}.
\end{equation}

As indicated by the coverage rates in \ref{tab:beetle_obstacle_trajectory}, conditions (A) and (B) allowed the robot to traverse most of the path, whereas under condition (C), the robot collided with an obstacle midway and the experiment was terminated.

In the velocity-based command generation method described in \ref{subsec:velmode}, the robot’s target velocity is determined based on the displacement from the device origin defined at the start of operation.  
Under condition (C), without force feedback, the operator could not reliably recognize the device origin.  
As a result, the robot unintentionally moved in the $-y$ direction and collided with an obstacle.  
This result demonstrates that force feedback is essential for safe navigation in an effectively unbounded workspace.

Furthermore, the RMSE in \ref{tab:beetle_obstacle_trajectory} is slightly smaller under condition (A) than under condition (B).  
As shown in \ref{fig:beetle_obstacle_plot}, the trajectory under condition (A) follows the ideal path (black dashed line) with oscillations, whereas under condition (B), noticeable deviations occur near $(x, y) = (0.3, -1.0)$ and during motion in the positive $y$ direction.

The first-person view provided to the operator has a limited field of view, as shown in the lower-left image of \ref{fig:beetle_obstacle_overview}, making it difficult to perceive lateral distances to obstacles.  
Therefore, under condition (A), the operator appears to have relied on the distance-based force feedback described in \ref{subsec:feedback_from_obstacle} to maintain a trajectory close to the ideal path.

However, when the operator attempted to move the robot toward an obstacle, the feedback described in \ref{subsec:feedback_in_velmode} and that described in \ref{subsec:feedback_from_obstacle} were generated in the same direction.  
Consequently, the operator could not distinguish whether the presented force resulted from issuing a motion command or from approaching an obstacle.  
Although the operator’s reaction—moving away from the obstacle—ensures collision avoidance, improving control transparency requires an additional mechanism to distinguish between these types of feedback.

The evaluation metrics shown in \ref{tab:beetle_cleaning_result_wall} were computed from the binarized images presented in the lower section of \ref{fig:beetle_cleaning_result_wall}.

The mean lateral distance is defined as the average of the shortest distances between each pixel in the painted region and each pixel belonging to the red guide line. 
This metric indicates the proximity of the painting trajectory to the guide line; a smaller value corresponds to closer alignment.

The angular deviation is defined as the angle between the principal direction vector of the painted region, obtained via principal component analysis (PCA), and the guide line. 
This metric evaluates the global alignment of the painting trajectory with the intended direction; a smaller value indicates better directional consistency.

The standard deviation (STD) of the local angular deviation is calculated by dividing the evaluation region into five sections perpendicular to the guide line, computing the angular deviation within each section, and then calculating the standard deviation of these values. 
This metric represents the degree of local undulation in the painting trajectory; a smaller value indicates smoother and more uniform motion.

This behavior is attributed to the difficulty of perceiving the contact state of the robot’s end-effector using only the visual feedback from the onboard camera.

In contrast, under condition (A), the painting trajectory remained continuous, and the contact force $F_x$ did not drop to zero. 
Although oscillations in $F_x$ were observed, these are considered to reflect the operator’s intentional modulation of the applied force, i.e., decreasing the force in response to increased reaction force and increasing it when the reaction force decreased.

These results indicate that the proposed haptic feedback system contributed to stabilizing contact-based manipulation during the remote operation of the flying robot.

The angular deviation is larger in condition (A) than in condition (B), whereas the standard deviation of the local angular deviation is smaller in condition (A) than in condition (B). 
These results indicate that although condition (A) exhibits lower global tracking accuracy relative to condition (B), the painting trajectory under condition (A) is locally more stable. 
This suggests that the proposed haptic feedback method contributes to stabilizing remote contact operations even in a base-fixed robotic system.

However, \ref{fig:me6_cleaning_result_plot} shows multiple instances of abrupt wrench variations in both conditions (A) and (B). 
These abrupt changes are attributed to the time delay between the operator’s input motion and the moment when the robot’s end-effector reaches the commanded position. 
Due to this delay, there is also a lag between the occurrence of the reaction force at the wall surface and the moment when the corresponding wrench is fed back to the operator. 
As a result, a sudden large force can be generated at the end-effector, and the delayed feedback force may push the operator’s hand backward, thereby destabilizing the contact.

This phenomenon was not observed in the previous experiment using the flying robot. 
The difference is considered to arise from the manner in which reaction forces are compensated. 
In the flying robot with an unfixed base, the reaction force corresponding to the end-effector force must be compensated through thrust control, making abrupt changes in end-effector force physically difficult. 
In contrast, the base-fixed robot used in this experiment is rigidly connected to the ground. 
If the joints possess sufficient stiffness, the reaction force at the base can statically compensate for the reaction generated at the end-effector. 
This static compensation enables rapid variations in end-effector force.

These results suggest that compensation strategies that explicitly account for time delays are necessary to achieve more stable contact-based manipulation.
